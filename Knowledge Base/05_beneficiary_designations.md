# Beneficiary Designations

## What is a Beneficiary?

A beneficiary is the person or entity designated to receive the death benefit of a life insurance policy when the insured passes away. Beneficiary designations are a critical component of a life insurance policy and should be reviewed regularly to ensure they reflect the policyholder's current wishes.

## Types of Beneficiaries

**Primary beneficiary:** The first in line to receive the death benefit. If the primary beneficiary is living at the time of the insured's death, they receive the full benefit (or their designated share if multiple primary beneficiaries are named).

**Contingent beneficiary (secondary beneficiary):** Receives the death benefit only if the primary beneficiary has predeceased the insured or is otherwise unable to receive the benefit. It is strongly recommended to name at least one contingent beneficiary.

**Tertiary beneficiary:** A third-level beneficiary, named in the event both primary and contingent beneficiaries are unavailable. Less common but available on some policies.

## Who Can Be Named as a Beneficiary?

Policyholders may designate:
- **Individuals:** Spouse, children, parents, siblings, or any other person.
- **Trusts:** Useful for estate planning purposes, particularly when beneficiaries are minors or have special needs.
- **Estates:** The death benefit is paid to the insured's estate and distributed according to the will or state law. Generally not recommended as it subjects the benefit to probate.
- **Charities or organizations:** Non-profit organizations or other entities can be named as beneficiaries.

## Revocable vs. Irrevocable Beneficiaries

**Revocable beneficiary:** The policyholder can change the beneficiary designation at any time without the beneficiary's consent. This is the most common arrangement.

**Irrevocable beneficiary:** The policyholder cannot change the beneficiary designation without the irrevocable beneficiary's written consent. This arrangement is sometimes used in divorce settlements, business agreements, or as collateral for a loan.

## Naming Multiple Beneficiaries

Multiple beneficiaries can be named at the primary or contingent level. The policyholder specifies how the death benefit should be divided, typically by percentage. Percentages must total 100%.

Example: 50% to spouse, 25% to child A, 25% to child B.

If a named beneficiary predeceases the insured and no contingent is named, the benefit may pass by default to the remaining primary beneficiaries, the estate, or according to the policy's terms.

## How to Update a Beneficiary Designation

Beneficiary changes are made by submitting a completed change of beneficiary form to the insurance company. Most companies require:
- The policy number
- The insured's signature
- The new beneficiary's full legal name, date of birth, relationship to insured, and Social Security number

Changes take effect when processed by the insurance company. A policy document or confirmation letter is typically issued.

**Important:** A will does not override a beneficiary designation on a life insurance policy. The named beneficiary on the policy controls who receives the death benefit, regardless of what the will states.

## Common Situations That Warrant a Beneficiary Review

- Marriage or divorce
- Birth or adoption of a child
- Death of a named beneficiary
- Significant changes in financial circumstances
- Changes in estate planning goals

## Common Customer Questions

**What happens if I don't name a beneficiary?**
If no beneficiary is named, or if all named beneficiaries have predeceased the insured, the death benefit is typically paid to the insured's estate. This can result in delays and may subject the benefit to probate proceedings.

**Can a minor be named as a beneficiary?**
Yes, but a minor cannot legally receive a large sum of money directly. The funds may be held by a court-appointed custodian until the minor reaches the age of majority. To avoid this, policyholders may name a trust or a custodian under the Uniform Transfers to Minors Act (UTMA).

**How long does it take to process a beneficiary change?**
Processing times vary by company but are typically completed within 5 to 10 business days of receiving a completed request.
