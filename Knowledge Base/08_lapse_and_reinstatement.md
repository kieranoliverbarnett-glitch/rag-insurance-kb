# Policy Lapse and Reinstatement

## What is a Policy Lapse?

A life insurance policy lapses when it goes out of force due to non-payment of premiums. Once a policy lapses, coverage ends and the death benefit is no longer payable. For term life policies, a lapse means coverage is lost entirely. For permanent policies with accumulated cash value, the policy may automatically continue under a nonforfeiture option before fully lapsing.

## The Grace Period

Most life insurance policies include a grace period — typically 30 or 31 days from the premium due date — during which a missed payment can be made without the policy lapsing. If the insured dies during the grace period, the death benefit is generally still payable, though the overdue premium may be deducted from the benefit.

If payment is not received by the end of the grace period, the policy lapses.

## Consequences of a Lapse

- **Loss of coverage:** The death benefit is no longer in force.
- **Loss of insurability protection:** If the insured's health has changed since the original policy was issued, reinstating or obtaining new coverage may be more difficult or more expensive.
- **Loss of original premium rate:** A reinstated or newly issued policy will be priced at the insured's current age and health status.
- **For permanent policies:** Accumulated cash value may be used to extend coverage under nonforfeiture provisions, but this is finite.

## What is Reinstatement?

Reinstatement is the process of restoring a lapsed policy to active status. Not all lapsed policies are eligible for reinstatement. Most carriers allow reinstatement within a defined window after lapse, subject to specific requirements.

## Reinstatement Eligibility

To be eligible for reinstatement, the following conditions typically apply:

- The reinstatement request must be submitted within the carrier's reinstatement window (commonly within 3 to 5 years of the lapse date).
- All overdue premiums must be paid, often with interest.
- The policyholder must submit a reinstatement application.
- Evidence of insurability (medical underwriting) may be required, depending on the time elapsed since lapse and the carrier's guidelines.

## The Reinstatement Process

1. **Contact the insurance company** to confirm reinstatement eligibility and obtain the required forms.
2. **Submit a reinstatement application.** The application will ask about changes in the insured's health since the policy was originally issued.
3. **Provide evidence of insurability if required.** This may involve answering health questions, completing a medical questionnaire, or undergoing a paramedical exam.
4. **Pay all overdue premiums**, typically with interest calculated from the lapse date.
5. **Await underwriting review.** The insurance company reviews the application and determines whether reinstatement is approved, approved with a modified rating, or declined.

## Reinstatement vs. Applying for a New Policy

In most cases, reinstatement is preferable to applying for a new policy because:

- The original issue age and health classification are restored upon reinstatement, which typically results in lower premiums than a new policy issued at an older age.
- The original policy terms, riders, and benefits are preserved.
- The original contestability period does not restart in most states (though some states and carriers differ — the reinstated policy may be contestable for a new two-year period from the reinstatement date).

## Common Customer Questions

**How long do I have to reinstate my policy?**
Reinstatement windows vary by carrier and state. Most carriers allow reinstatement within 3 to 5 years of the lapse date. After this window closes, reinstatement is no longer an option and a new policy would need to be applied for.

**Will I need a medical exam to reinstate?**
It depends on how long the policy has been lapsed and the carrier's guidelines. A reinstatement within a short period after lapse (e.g., within 6 months) may require only a signed health statement. A longer lapse period typically requires more extensive evidence of insurability.

**Do I have to pay all the missed premiums at once?**
Generally yes. All overdue premiums, plus interest, must be paid as a condition of reinstatement. Some carriers may allow a payment arrangement, but this is not universal.

**What if I am declined for reinstatement?**
If reinstatement is declined due to health issues, the policy cannot be restored. The policyholder may need to explore other coverage options, which may be limited or more expensive depending on their current health status.
