# The Reinstatement Process

## Overview

Reinstatement is the formal process of restoring a lapsed life insurance policy to active status. When a policy lapses due to non-payment of premiums, coverage ends — but most insurance companies provide a window during which the policyholder can apply to have the policy reinstated rather than applying for entirely new coverage.

This article covers the reinstatement process in detail, including eligibility, required steps, underwriting requirements, and important timelines.

## Eligibility Requirements

To be eligible for reinstatement, the following conditions generally apply:

- **The request must be made within the reinstatement window.** Most carriers allow reinstatement for up to 3 to 5 years from the date of lapse. After this window closes, reinstatement is no longer available and the policyholder would need to apply for a new policy.
- **The policy must not have been surrendered.** A policy that was formally surrendered for its cash value cannot be reinstated.
- **All overdue premiums must be paid.** The policyholder must pay all premiums that were missed during the lapse period, typically with interest.

## The Reinstatement Application

Reinstatement is not automatic. It requires the policyholder to submit a formal reinstatement application to the insurance company. The application will ask the policyholder to:

- Confirm the policy to be reinstated (policy number, insured's name).
- Disclose any changes in the insured's health since the policy was originally issued or since the last time the policy was in force.
- Authorize any medical records review if required.
- Sign the application attesting that the information provided is accurate and complete.

The insured's health disclosures on the reinstatement application are reviewed by the insurance company's underwriting team. Misrepresentation on a reinstatement application carries the same consequences as misrepresentation on an original application.

## Medical Underwriting Requirements

The extent of medical underwriting required for reinstatement depends on how long the policy has been lapsed:

**Short lapse period (typically within 6 months):**
A simplified health statement may be sufficient. The policyholder answers a limited set of health questions, and the carrier typically does not require a paramedical exam.

**Longer lapse period (typically 6 months to 5 years):**
Full evidence of insurability is generally required. This may include:
- A detailed health questionnaire
- Attending physician statements
- A paramedical exam (blood draw, urinalysis, blood pressure measurement)
- Review of medical records

If the insured's health has significantly declined since the original policy was issued, reinstatement may be declined or approved at a higher premium rate.

## Payment of Overdue Premiums and Interest

As a condition of reinstatement, the policyholder must pay all premiums that went unpaid during the lapse period, plus interest. The interest rate is typically specified in the policy contract and is calculated from the date of each missed premium to the reinstatement date.

The total amount due — including back premiums and interest — must generally be paid in full before or at the time of reinstatement approval. Some carriers may allow a payment arrangement in limited circumstances, but this is not standard.

## Underwriting Review and Decision

Once the reinstatement application and payment are received, the underwriting team reviews the submission and issues one of the following decisions:

- **Approved:** The policy is reinstated on the original terms and at the original premium rate (adjusted only for any applicable rate changes or age-based recalculation per policy terms).
- **Approved with modification:** The policy is reinstated but at a higher premium or with a modified rating due to changes in the insured's health.
- **Declined:** Reinstatement is denied due to the insured's current health status. The policyholder would need to explore alternative coverage options.

## New Contestability Period

An important consideration with reinstatement is the potential restart of the contestability period. Depending on state law and the carrier's policy:

- Some states require a new two-year contestability period to begin from the reinstatement date.
- Other states allow the original contestability period to apply, provided it has already elapsed.

Policyholders should ask their carrier directly about whether reinstatement will trigger a new contestability period.

## Why Reinstatement is Often Preferable to a New Policy

Reinstating a lapsed policy is generally more advantageous than applying for a new policy because:

- The original issue age is preserved, which typically results in a lower premium than a new policy issued at the insured's current age.
- Original policy terms, riders, and benefits are restored.
- If the insured has developed health conditions since the original policy was issued, reinstatement (if approved) allows them to maintain coverage they might not qualify for at a standard rate under a new application.

## Common Customer Questions

**How do I start the reinstatement process?**
Contact the insurance company's customer service team with the policy number. A representative will confirm whether the policy is eligible for reinstatement, provide the required forms, and advise on the total amount due for back premiums and interest.

**How long does reinstatement take?**
The timeline depends on the underwriting requirements. A simplified reinstatement may be processed within a few days. A full underwriting review — including a paramedical exam and medical records — can take 4 to 8 weeks or longer.

**Can I reinstate a policy if I was the one who requested the lapse?**
Yes, provided the policy was not formally surrendered and the reinstatement window has not closed. Requesting a lapse (i.e., stopping premium payments) does not preclude reinstatement.

**What if I cannot afford to pay all the back premiums at once?**
Most carriers require full payment of back premiums and interest as a condition of reinstatement. If this is not possible, the policyholder may need to explore alternative coverage options. It is worth contacting the carrier to ask about available options, as policies and exceptions vary.

**Will my original beneficiary designations still be in place?**
Yes. Reinstating a policy restores the policy to its last active state, including beneficiary designations. However, it is a good practice to review and confirm beneficiary information at the time of reinstatement.
