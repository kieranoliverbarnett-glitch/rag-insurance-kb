# EFT and Payment Setup

## What is EFT?

EFT stands for Electronic Funds Transfer. In the context of life insurance, EFT refers to the automatic deduction of premium payments directly from a policyholder's bank account on a scheduled basis. EFT is also commonly referred to as automatic bank draft or auto-pay.

## Benefits of EFT

- **Convenience:** Premiums are paid automatically, reducing the risk of missed payments due to oversight.
- **Policy protection:** Consistent, on-time payments help ensure the policy remains in good standing and does not lapse.
- **Premium discount:** Many insurance companies offer a small premium discount for policyholders who enroll in EFT, as it reduces administrative processing costs.
- **Flexible frequency:** EFT can typically be set up on a monthly, quarterly, semi-annual, or annual basis depending on the policy and carrier.

## Setting Up EFT

To enroll in EFT, the policyholder typically needs to provide:

- Bank account type (checking or savings)
- Bank routing number (9-digit number identifying the financial institution)
- Bank account number
- Preferred draft date (the day of the month the payment will be deducted)
- Signature authorizing the automatic withdrawal

Most carriers accept EFT enrollment by phone, online, or through a paper authorization form. A voided check may be required in some cases to verify banking information.

## Changing EFT Information

Policyholders can update their EFT information at any time. Common reasons for updating include:

- Opening a new bank account
- Changing banks
- Updating account numbers due to a lost or stolen card (for debit-linked accounts)

Changes typically require a new EFT authorization form and may take one billing cycle to take effect. Policyholders should ensure their current payment method remains active until the change is confirmed to avoid a missed payment.

## Removing EFT

If a policyholder wants to discontinue EFT, they must notify the insurance company in advance of the next scheduled draft date. Upon cancellation of EFT, the policyholder is responsible for submitting premium payments manually by the due date. Failure to do so within the grace period may result in a policy lapse.

## Draft Date

The draft date is the day of the month on which the EFT payment is deducted from the bank account. Most carriers allow policyholders to choose a draft date within a range of available options. Changing the draft date requires written or verbal authorization and typically takes effect the following billing cycle.

**Important:** When EFT is removed from a policy, the draft date on file becomes inactive. If EFT is later re-enrolled, a new draft date must be established.

## What Happens if an EFT Payment Fails?

If an EFT draft is returned due to insufficient funds or an account issue, the insurance company will typically notify the policyholder. The policy enters a grace period during which payment must be received to keep the policy in force. Repeated failed drafts may result in the carrier removing EFT privileges.

## Common Customer Questions

**How long does it take for EFT to activate?**
EFT enrollment typically takes 5 to 10 business days to process. The first draft under a new EFT setup may occur in the following billing cycle.

**Can I choose which day my payment drafts?**
Yes, in most cases. Available draft dates vary by carrier but commonly include dates between the 1st and 28th of the month.

**Will I receive a confirmation when my EFT payment processes?**
Policies vary. Some carriers send a confirmation notice; others reflect the payment in the policyholder's online account or annual statement. The transaction will also appear on the policyholder's bank statement.

**What if my bank account changes before my next draft?**
Update your EFT information as soon as possible to avoid a failed payment. Contact the insurance company directly to submit new banking information before the scheduled draft date.
