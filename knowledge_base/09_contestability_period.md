# The Contestability Period

## What is the Contestability Period?

The contestability period is a defined window of time — typically the first two years a life insurance policy is in force — during which the insurance company has the right to investigate and potentially deny a death claim based on material misrepresentations made on the original application.

The contestability period exists to protect insurance companies from fraud while still providing policyholders with a clear endpoint after which the policy becomes incontestable.

## How It Works

When a life insurance application is submitted, the applicant answers a series of questions about their health history, lifestyle, and other risk factors. The insurance company uses this information to assess risk and determine the premium rate.

If the insured dies during the contestability period, the insurance company may:

1. Pay the claim promptly if no concerns are identified.
2. Open an investigation to review the original application for accuracy.
3. Deny the claim or adjust the benefit if a material misrepresentation is found.

After the contestability period ends, the policy is considered incontestable. The insurance company generally cannot deny a death claim based on application misrepresentations, with limited exceptions (such as outright fraud in some states).

## What is a Material Misrepresentation?

A material misrepresentation is a false or misleading statement on the application that would have affected the insurance company's decision to issue the policy or the premium charged. Examples include:

- Failing to disclose a diagnosed medical condition
- Providing inaccurate information about tobacco use
- Omitting a history of a serious illness or treatment
- Providing incorrect information about age or occupation

Not every inaccuracy constitutes a material misrepresentation. Minor or unintentional errors that would not have changed the underwriting outcome may not be sufficient grounds for a claim denial.

## Suicide Exclusion

Most life insurance policies include a separate suicide exclusion clause, typically also covering the first two years the policy is in force. If the insured dies by suicide during this period, the death benefit may not be paid. After the exclusion period, death by suicide is generally covered under the policy.

## Does the Contestability Period Ever Restart?

Yes, in certain circumstances the contestability period can restart:

- **Policy reinstatement:** When a lapsed policy is reinstated, a new contestability period may begin from the reinstatement date. This varies by state law and carrier policy.
- **Policy conversion:** Converting a term policy to a permanent policy may trigger a new contestability period for the converted policy, depending on the carrier.
- **Increases in coverage:** Adding a rider or increasing the death benefit may subject the new coverage amount to a new contestability period.

## What Happens After the Contestability Period?

Once the contestability period ends, the policy is incontestable. This means:

- The insurance company cannot investigate the original application for misrepresentations.
- Death claims are generally paid without an underwriting review of the application.
- The policyholder has a high degree of certainty that the death benefit will be paid as long as premiums are current.

## Common Customer Questions

**Can my claim be denied after two years for any reason?**
After the contestability period, claims can still be denied for reasons unrelated to application accuracy — such as policy lapse due to non-payment, an active exclusion (e.g., death during an excluded activity), or fraud specifically defined in the policy. However, the insurer cannot contest the original application.

**What if I made an honest mistake on my application?**
If an innocent error is discovered during a contestability investigation, the outcome depends on whether the error was material. If the information would not have changed the underwriting decision, the claim is generally paid. If it would have resulted in a higher premium, the insurance company may adjust the benefit proportionally rather than deny it outright.

**Does the contestability period apply to all types of life insurance?**
The two-year contestability period is standard across term, whole life, and universal life policies. The specific terms are governed by state insurance law and the policy contract.
