# Accelerated Death Benefits

## What is an Accelerated Death Benefit?

An Accelerated Death Benefit (ADB) is a life insurance policy feature that allows a terminally or chronically ill policyholder to receive a portion of their death benefit while still alive. Rather than waiting for the death benefit to be paid to beneficiaries after death, the policyholder can access funds to help cover medical expenses, long-term care costs, or other financial needs during a serious illness.

Accelerated Death Benefits are sometimes called "living benefits."

## How Does It Work?

When a qualifying condition is diagnosed, the policyholder submits a claim for an accelerated benefit. If approved, the insurance company pays out a portion of the policy's death benefit — commonly between 25% and 100%, depending on the policy terms and the nature of the qualifying condition.

The amount accelerated is deducted from the total death benefit. The remaining death benefit continues in force and will be paid to the beneficiary upon the insured's death.

**Example:** A policyholder has a $500,000 death benefit and accelerates $200,000 due to a terminal illness. The remaining death benefit available to beneficiaries upon death is $300,000 (less any applicable interest or administrative charges).

## Qualifying Conditions

ADB eligibility and qualifying conditions vary by policy and carrier. Common qualifying categories include:

**Terminal illness:** The insured has been diagnosed with a condition expected to result in death within a defined timeframe — typically 12 to 24 months, depending on the policy.

**Chronic illness:** The insured is unable to perform a specified number of Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) — such as bathing, dressing, eating, or mobility — without assistance, or requires substantial supervision due to severe cognitive impairment.

**Critical illness:** Some policies cover specific diagnoses such as heart attack, stroke, cancer, organ failure, or ALS. This varies significantly by policy.

## ADB as a Rider vs. Built-In Feature

Some policies include an accelerated death benefit as a standard feature at no additional cost. Others offer it as an optional rider that can be added to the policy for an additional premium. Policyholders should review their policy or contact their carrier to confirm whether ADB is included and under what terms.

## Tax Considerations

Accelerated death benefits paid due to a terminal illness diagnosis are generally income tax-free under federal law, provided the insured is certified as terminally ill by a licensed physician. Benefits paid for chronic illness may also qualify for favorable tax treatment but are subject to per diem limitations set by the IRS. Tax treatment can be complex and may vary based on individual circumstances.

## Impact on the Policy

When an accelerated benefit is paid:

- The remaining death benefit is reduced by the amount accelerated, plus any applicable interest or charges.
- Premiums may be reduced proportionally, or the policyholder may continue paying the original premium on the reduced benefit.
- The cash value of a permanent policy (if applicable) may also be reduced.

## Common Customer Questions

**Will using the accelerated benefit affect my beneficiaries?**
Yes. The death benefit paid to beneficiaries will be reduced by the amount that was accelerated, plus any fees or interest charged. It is important for policyholders to discuss this with their beneficiaries before filing an ADB claim.

**How long does the ADB claims process take?**
Processing times vary by carrier, but most carriers require a physician certification of the qualifying condition before approving an accelerated benefit. The process can take several weeks from the time the request is submitted.

**Can I accelerate the full death benefit?**
Some policies allow acceleration of up to 100% of the death benefit for terminal illness. Others cap the acceleration at a lower percentage. The maximum amount available is defined in the policy or rider terms.

**Is an ADB the same as long-term care insurance?**
No, though they can serve a similar function. Long-term care insurance is a separate product designed specifically to cover the costs of ongoing care. An ADB on a life insurance policy provides a lump sum or periodic benefit drawn from the death benefit, which may be used for long-term care expenses but is not a dedicated long-term care product.
