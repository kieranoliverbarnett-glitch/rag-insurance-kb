# Annuities

## What is an Annuity?

An annuity is a financial product issued by an insurance company that provides a series of future payments in exchange for a present-day deposit or series of deposits. Annuities are primarily used as a retirement income tool, offering a way to convert accumulated savings into a predictable income stream.

Contributions grow on a tax-deferred basis during the accumulation phase. When the policyholder is ready to receive income, the policy enters the distribution phase.

## The Two Phases of an Annuity

**Accumulation phase:** The period during which the annuity owner contributes money and the funds grow tax-deferred. No income taxes are owed on growth until funds are withdrawn.

**Distribution phase:** The period during which the insurance company makes regular payments to the annuity owner. Taxes may be owed on withdrawals depending on whether the annuity was funded with pre-tax or after-tax dollars.

## Types of Annuities

### Fixed Annuities
A fixed annuity provides a guaranteed minimum interest rate and predictable, stable growth. The insurance company sets the interest rate, which does not fluctuate with market conditions. Fixed annuities are considered the most conservative annuity option and are well suited for individuals who prioritize income stability over growth potential.

### Variable Annuities
Variable annuities allow the owner to allocate contributions among a selection of investment sub-accounts, similar to mutual funds. Growth potential is higher than fixed annuities, but the cash value can decrease if the chosen investments perform poorly. Variable annuities are securities products and carry investment risk.

### Indexed Annuities
Indexed annuities link cash value growth to the performance of a market index, such as the S&P 500. They typically include:
- A **floor** (often 0%), which protects against market losses.
- A **cap**, which limits the maximum gain in a given period even if the index performs above the cap.

Indexed annuities offer a middle ground between the security of fixed annuities and the growth potential of variable annuities.

### Immediate Annuities
An immediate annuity begins distributing payments within one year of the initial premium. It is almost always funded with a single lump-sum contribution. Immediate annuities are well suited for individuals who are already in or near retirement and want to convert a lump sum into income right away.

### Deferred Annuities
A deferred annuity delays the distribution of payments until a future date specified in the contract. During the accumulation period, contributions grow tax-deferred. Deferred annuities can be funded with a single premium or a series of contributions over time.

## Premium Structure

**Single-premium annuity:** Funded with one lump-sum payment. Common with immediate annuities and some deferred products.

**Flexible-premium annuity:** Allows the owner to make contributions over time, in varying amounts and on a flexible schedule.

## Surrender Charges

Most deferred annuities include a surrender charge period, typically ranging from 3 to 10 years. Withdrawing funds during this period may result in a surrender charge assessed by the insurance company. Most contracts allow a free withdrawal of a portion of the account value (commonly 10%) each year without penalty.

## Tax Considerations

- Annuity growth is tax-deferred during the accumulation phase.
- Withdrawals are subject to ordinary income tax on the gain portion.
- Withdrawals taken before age 59½ may be subject to a 10% IRS early withdrawal penalty in addition to income taxes.
- Annuities held inside a qualified retirement account (IRA, 401k) are subject to required minimum distribution rules.

## Common Customer Questions

**Is an annuity the same as life insurance?**
No. While both are issued by insurance companies, an annuity is designed to provide income during the owner's lifetime, whereas life insurance is designed to provide a benefit upon the owner's death.

**What happens to my annuity when I die?**
Most annuities include a death benefit. The remaining account value or a minimum guaranteed amount is typically paid to the named beneficiary. Specific terms vary by contract.

**Can I access my money before the distribution phase?**
Yes, but withdrawals during the accumulation phase may be subject to surrender charges and tax consequences. Most contracts allow a free annual withdrawal of up to 10% of the account value.
